I decided I'd put off building a personal website for myself for far too long, so I gave myself one weekend to get it done.

Goals

My goals were to have a site that:

  • is simple to update that won't "get in the way" of me blogging
  • allows me to focus on and practice my front-end skills
  • has a visual style that reflects my personality
  • promotes my professional work

Design

I wanted the design to be simple, that wouldn't take too long to create so that I could design and build it within the weekend, and that reflected my personality.

I decided to do some research and look at other sites that I wanted to be on a par with content wise, or that I liked the visual design of. I like typography and simple monotone colours, and I wanted the images on my site to be sketched - I sketch things all the time at work and for fun, so I felt it was an important to use sketches in my website design.

I looked at a few different sites but the following three sites influenced my website design the most.

Hand drawn sketches on Learn You a Haskell

I had stumbled across Learn You a Haskell previously and I liked the cutsy style of the sketches.

Large typographic headings on Fathom.info

I came across the Fathom site as I have a love/hate relationship with infographics (i.e. I love good ones, hate bad ones - of which there are many!). I like the huge size of the heading, and I also like that the site is all greyscale.

Monotone colours on squaredeye.com

The design of Squared Eye is busier than I wanted personally, but I like the pale colours, the illustrations and the textured feel.

I started to look at fonts but found myself going down a rabbit hole. Seeing as I wanted to complete the project in a weekend I decided to keep it simple and stick with Courier and Helvetica for now.

I like sci-fi films and love drawing spaceships, aliens and robots, so as part of reflecting my personality I was keen to include the space fighter jets as part of my design. My website is very simple with large headings, but the fighter jets add a bit of fun and recognisable visual branding that I can apply to other collateral.

I went with very simple grey colours as I plan to include photos and colourful images in the future. I think this style will work as I develop my site and add more imagery in the content.

Technical bits

I decided to use Jekyll as it didn't require me to learn any programming, but I can still style as I like. It's really easy to install and I can use HTML, Markdown, or Textile for pages and blog posts. An added bonus is that I can host it on GitHub (as Jekyll is the engine behind GitHub pages) which means I can practice using Git which previously I was using very infrequently and thus forgetting commands.

The end result

I have a site that I designed and built and published live in a weekend. I am able to improve it easily bit by bit when I have time. Though it has a very simple minimal design, it still reflects my personality, and it's easy to maintain.
